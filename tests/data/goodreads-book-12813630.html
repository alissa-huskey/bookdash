At once a viscerally raw horror thriller, a YA paranormal romance, a pandemic apocalypse despotic, a political satire, and even a touch of a mystery. So, not your typical vampire story.

I loved Tana as a protagonist because she is neither a damsel in distress, nor an action hero, but a survivor. Almost every moment of the story she is nearly paralyzed with fear, yet in spite of that she is determined and clever. She doesn't romanticize monsters and conveniently forget the dark pasts of cute bad boys. Instead she's compassionate in a way that feels like she sees people and monsters in all their complexity, neither forgiving foibles for virtues nor dismissing all redeeming qualities in the face of transgression. Being able to really see people for who they are is key to her survival, but it also makes her kind. Plus she's sardonic with a self-deprecating inner dialog, which, y'know, I'm into.

I will say, I was a bit put off of the book at first. I got a bit of a Black Mirror vibe what with its vampire hunter reality TV shows and social media celebrity fang-bangers, and I only like that sort of black social commentary in small doses. But it turned out to be a moderately employed and well executed part of a much bigger picture.

I've been binging Holly Black's books lately and I've come to love her writing style, in this book in particular. Her quotes at the beginning of each chapter are beautiful and often eerily fitting. The world feels tangible and the characters real and flawed and full of personality. There are so many moments that surprised me and made me smile--a great word choice, a hauntingly lyrical quote, a witty piece of dialog, a plot twist I didn't see coming.

Much more to this book than I would have expected, and much more of it that I liked. ...more
